Facilitator Self-Care Guide

*You cannot pour from an empty cup. Peer support work is emotionally demanding — taking care of yourself isn't optional, it's part of the job.*

Know the signs of compassion fatigue

If you're experiencing several of these, it's time to adjust:

  • **Irritability** — small things feel like big things
  • **Numbness** — you stop feeling moved by others' stories
  • **Avoidance** — you dread sessions or find reasons to cancel
  • **Intrusive thoughts** — carrying participants' stories home with you
  • **Physical symptoms** — headaches, sleep changes, fatigue
  • **Cynicism** — "nothing ever changes anyway"

Boundaries to set

With participants

  • Sessions start and end on time
  • You are not available between sessions (except for emergencies with a clear plan)
  • You share your own experiences intentionally, not reactively
  • You refer out when someone needs professional therapy or case management

With yourself

  • You do not answer emails or messages about the group outside of working hours
  • You keep a separate journal for your own processing (not session notes)
  • You have at least one person you can talk to about the work (supervisor, peer, therapist)

With your co-facilitator

  • Debrief after every session — 10 minutes minimum
  • Check in on each other: "How are you holding up?"
  • Have a signal for "I need you to take the lead right now"
  • Rotate difficult tasks (crisis follow-ups, intake calls)

Daily practices

Before a session

  • 5 minutes of quiet or grounding
  • Review the session plan
  • Check in with your co-facilitator
  • Set an intention ("I will listen without trying to fix")

After a session

  • Write session notes (within 24 hours)
  • Do a brief transition ritual (short walk, drink of water, change of clothes)
  • Debrief with co-facilitator
  • Let go of what is not yours to carry

Weekly

  • Review the week's sessions — any themes you're carrying?
  • One activity that has nothing to do with peer support
  • Connect with someone who understands the work

When to step back

  • You dread sessions for more than two weeks
  • Your own mental health is declining
  • You're showing up but not present
  • A participant's story is triggering your own unprocessed experience
  • Someone you trust tells you they're worried about you

Stepping back is not failure. It is the most responsible thing you can do for yourself and the people you support.

*This guide is informed by the work of Laurie Anne Pearlman, Charles Figley, and the Center for Victims of Torture's self-care resources.*
